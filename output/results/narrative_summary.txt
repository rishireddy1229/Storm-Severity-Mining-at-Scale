STORM SEVERITY MINING AT SCALE — KEY FINDINGS
==================================================

Dataset: 1,708,366 storm events across 69 states (1996-2024)
Total economic damage: $628.6B
Total deaths: 22,909
Total injuries: 101,328

--- INSIGHT: Extreme Damage Skewness ---
76.4% of all events record zero economic damage.
Among damage-positive events, the top 1% account for $568.3B (90.4% of total damage).
This extreme right-skew (skewness=148) motivates log-transforming damage before quartile binning for classification.

Most economically damaging event types:
  - Hurricane (Typhoon): $167.2B (26.6% of total)
  - Flash Flood: $102.7B (16.3% of total)
  - Storm Surge/Tide: $61.0B (9.7% of total)

Deadliest event types:
  - Heat: 3,834 deaths
  - Excessive Heat: 2,611 deaths
  - Tornado: 2,175 deaths

--- INSIGHT: Economic vs Human Cost Divergence ---
The most economically damaging type (Hurricane (Typhoon)) differs from the deadliest (Heat). This mismatch implies that damage mitigation and casualty prevention require different resource allocation strategies.

Worst year for damage: 2005 ($100.7B)
Most damaging season: Summer ($262.0B)

--- INSIGHT: Prediction Limitations ---
Random Forest achieves ROC-AUC=0.852 but accuracy=63.4% on 4-class damage prediction. The gap reflects that adjacent damage tiers (e.g., Medium vs High) share nearly identical event-level features. Geographic features (lat/lon) dominate importance, confirming that damage is spatially driven rather than meteorologically determined.

--- INSIGHT: Event Escalation Patterns ---
Sequential mining reveals Hail → Thunderstorm Wind as the dominant escalation pathway. The bidirectional nature (Hail ↔ TSW) suggests these are concurrent manifestations of the same convective system rather than true causal escalation.
